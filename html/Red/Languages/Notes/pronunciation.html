Accentuation

The last syllable of a Latin word is called the ultima, the second-last the penult, and the third-last the antepenult. Only the penult or the antepenult may be stressed. Thus, if a word has only two syllables, the penult is stressed. If a word has more than two syllables, then its stress accent is determined according to the law of the penult: If the penult is long, it is stressed. If the penult is short, the antepenult is stressed. A syllable is long if it contains a long vowel or a dipthong (long by nature), or a short vowel followed by two or more consonants not necessarily in the same syllable (long by position).

  • i is consonantal at the beginning of a word when followed by a vowel (Iu_no_), and in the middle of a word when it falls between two vowels (biiugis). It may also be consonantal in the middle of a compound (a word made with a root word and a prefix - iniustus = in- + iustus). An exception to this rule occurs in certain words borrowed from Greek, in which an initial i is pronounced as a vowel - iambus.

  • When double consonants occur, both consonants are pronounced, i.e. ille is pronounced "il - le".

Italian

Nearly all Italian words end in a vowel. Guttural and nasal sounds are not very strong in Italian. Most words are formed in the forward section of the mouth.

Vowels

e say; get

Accentuation

Most words are stressed on the penult. Many words are stressed on the antepenult. A few words are stressed on the ultima, which requires a written accent. Some words are stressed on the fourth-to-last syllable.

Spanish

In Spanish speech, all words between pauses are strung together in a 'breath group'. Within the breath group, certain consonants are softened (b, d, g), and when identical vowels or consonants are adjacent to each other, one of them is omitted.

  • Whether stressed or unstressed, the sounds of Spanish vowels are always clear, tense, and short. The stress doesn't change the sound of a vowel the way it does in English, e.g. in Spanish the three a's of Panama_' all have the same sound.
  • Avoid lengthening final vowels, as in the glided 'o' of the English 'no'. Vowel sounds in Spanish are always short.
