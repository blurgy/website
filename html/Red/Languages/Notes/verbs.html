Verbs

Latin

Every Latin verb has a

  • person (first, second, or third),
  • number (singular or plural),
  • tense (present, imperfect, future, perfect, pluperfect, future perfect),
  • voice (active or passive), and
  • mood (Indicative, Imperative, or Subjunctive)
The person denotes the relation of the speaker to the subject - whether the speaker is discussing himself, the listener, or a third subject. The number denotes the quantity of the subject. The tense is determined by the combination of 1. The time at which the action occurs with respect to the time of the speaker (present, past, or future), and 2. The relation of the action to the passage of time (simple, progressive, repeated, or completed) (also called the aspect of the verb). All Latin noun tenses that express progressive aspect may also express repeated aspect. The voice denotes whether the verb's subject is performing or receiving the action of the verb. The mood denotes the speaker's attitude toward the factuality or likelihood of the verb's action.

The Indicative Mood

The indicative mood is used to represent something as factual, or to ask a question of fact. There are six tenses of the indicative mood:

  • Present - Action in present time, with simple or progressive/repeated aspect. (She thinks. She is thinking. She thinks [every day / often / repeatedly].)
  • Imperfect - Action in past time, with progressive/repeated aspect. (She was thinking. She used to think. She thought [every day / often / repeatedly].)
  • Future - Action in future time, with simple or progressive/repeated aspect. (She will think. She will be thinking. She will think [every day / often / repeatedly].)
  • Perfect - 1. Action in present time, with completed aspect. (She has thought.) or 2. Action in past time, with simple aspect. (She thought.)
  • Pluperfect - Action in past time, with completed aspect. (She had thought.)
  • Future Perfect - Action in future time, with completed aspect. (She will have thought.)
