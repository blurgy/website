Latin Adjectives

Possessive Adjectives | Reflexive-Possessive Adjectives | Intensive Adjectives

Every Latin adjective has a

  • gender (masculine, feminine, or neuter),
  • number (singular or plural), and
  • case (nominative, genitive, dative, accusative, ablative, vocative, or locative).
The gender is determined by the gender of the noun the adjective modifies, the number by the quantity of the noun, and case by the function of the noun in the sentence. Hence the declension of a Latin adjective contains all genders.

Latin adjectives usually come after the nouns they modify, but adjectives of size or quantity often precede their nouns.

Possessive Adjectives

To denote an object's possessor in the first or second-person, a possessive adjective may be used to refer to the possessor, which differs depending on the quantity of person. All possessive adjectives belong to the first-second declension, and may precede or follow the nouns they modify:

First Person Singular - 'my'

First Person Plural - 'our'

Second Person Singular - 'your'

Second Person Plural - 'your'

When an object's possessor is in the third-person, the genitive of possession is used insead of a possessive adjective. Reflexive-Possessive Adjectives

When an object's possessor is the subject of the sentence's verb, a reflexive-possessive adjective may be used to refer to the possessor. The first- and second-person singular and plural forms are identical to those of the possessive adjectives above. But unlike the possessive adjectives, there is a third-person form, identical in singular and plural:

Third Person - 'his, her, its, their (own)'

Intensive Adjectives

An intensive adjective emphasizes or intensifies the noun in modifies.

The intensive adjective ipse may be translated "himself," "herself," "itself," or "themselves." If modifying the unexpressed subject of a verb, it may be translated "I myself," "He himself," "She herself," or "They themselves." It may serve to identify something in particular, in which case it is translated "very."
